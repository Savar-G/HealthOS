## Personalized Heart Rate Zones (Karvonen Method)

> **Formula:** Target HR = (HRR x % intensity) + Resting HR
>
> The Karvonen method is endorsed by the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) because it uses heart rate reserve rather than raw max HR, producing zones that correlate more closely with %VO2max and account for individual fitness level (Karvonen et al., 1957; ACSM Guidelines, 11th ed.).

| Zone | Name | % of HRR | HR Range | Perceived Effort | Purpose |

| **Zone 2** | Aerobic Base | 60-70% | 136-150 bpm | Easy, full sentences | Long runs, aerobic base building, run/walk sessions |

## Training Philosophy (Evidence-Based)

### Concurrent Training Integration

Savar runs a Push/Pull/Legs+Core/Upper split (4x/week strength) alongside 2x/week running. The running schedule is designed around the interference effect:

- **Tuesday (post-legs):** Zone 2 / easy running only. Acts as active recovery 16-20 hours after Monday Legs + Core. Low-intensity cardio post-resistance training promotes recovery without impairing strength adaptation (Doma et al., 2015). Legs may feel heavy initially — this clears within 8-10 minutes.
- **Friday (quality):** High-intensity running (tempo, intervals, sprints in Phase 2+). Full 4-day recovery from legs ensures neuromuscular system is fresh for hard efforts. Wilson et al. (2012) meta-analysis confirms interference is minimized when high-intensity running is separated from heavy lower-body training by 48+ hours.

The Monday Legs + Core session partially compensates for reduced running volume by maintaining lower-body neuromuscular stimulus, musculotendinous resilience, and running economy (Blagrove et al., 2018 meta-analysis: heavy resistance training improves running economy 2-8%).

### Monitoring: HR-Primary, RPE-Secondary

Research shows heart rate monitoring produces more consistent training outcomes than RPE alone, particularly for beginners who tend to misjudge effort (Eston & Williams, 1988). However, RPE (1-10 scale) serves as a valuable secondary check. When HR and RPE diverge, it signals fatigue, dehydration, heat stress, or illness.

**Protocol:** Log both avg HR and RPE (1-10) for every run. Over time, you'll calibrate your internal sense of effort against the objective HR data.

### Run/Walk Method (Phase 1)

Research on Galloway's run-walk-run method shows that structured walk breaks at the same average pace produce less post-exercise muscle damage than continuous running, with marathon completion rates of 98% in Galloway-coached cohorts. For a runner returning from injury at 1-2 mile capacity, run/walk intervals are the safest re-entry protocol.

**Your Phase 1 approach:** Start with Run 2 min / Walk 1 min intervals. Gradually extend run segments and reduce walk segments over 8 weeks until you reach 20-30 minutes of continuous running.

### The 80/20 Principle (Polarized Training)

Seiler's research and a 2025 study in *Scientific Reports* (120 recreational marathoners) confirms: 80% of training volume at low intensity (Zones 1-2) and 20% at high intensity (Zones 3-5) produces superior endurance gains.

**Important caveat:** A University of Verona trial found that below ~4 hours/week of training, intensity distribution has minimal impact. At 3 days/week, the 80/20 framework is a useful guide but consistency and progressive volume matter most.

**In your week (once past run/walk phase):**
- Tuesday (Zone 2 Long Run) = Zone 1-2 (aerobic base, post-legs active recovery)
- Friday (Quality Run) = Zone 3-5 (tempo, intervals, sprints)
- With 2 sessions, the split is 50/50 by session count but ~60-65% easy / 35-40% hard by training time. Research confirms this is effective at low weekly volumes (Foster et al., 2015).

### The 10% Rule: Applied to Your Longest Run

A landmark British Journal of Sports Medicine cohort study (5,200+ runners, 18 months) found that injury risk is most strongly tied to spikes in your single longest run, not weekly total mileage. Increasing your longest run >10% vs. the longest run in the previous 30 days correlates with 64% higher injury risk.

**Protocol:** Progress your Sunday long run by no more than 10% at a time. Weekly total can remain stable while the long run grows.

### 3-Week Build / 1-Week Recovery (Periodization)

Standard mesocycle structure supported by meta-analyses in strength and endurance training: 3 weeks of progressive loading followed by 1 recovery week (~25-30% volume reduction). Recovery weeks are when actual physiological adaptation occurs. They are non-negotiable.

### Zone 2 Is Your Foundation

12-16 weeks of consistent Zone 2 training increases mitochondrial volume by 15-20% (San Millan & Brooks research). Zone 2 builds mitochondrial density, fat oxidation, capillary networks, and cardiac efficiency.

**The talk test:** Full, easy sentences = Zone 2. Pausing mid-sentence to breathe = too hard.

### Strength and Injury Prevention

A 2024 systematic review in *Sports Medicine* found that hip/core-focused strength training significantly reduces running injury rates in those areas. Unsupervised general strength training showed minimal benefit. Targeted, intentional work matters.

**Recommendation:** 2x/week hip and core strength work on non-running days. Focus on single-leg squats, hip bridges, clamshells, planks, and dead bugs.

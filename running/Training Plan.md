# Savar's Half Marathon Training Plan

_Last updated: April 11, 2026 | Program start: April 14, 2026_

> **How this plan works:** This is a dynamic, rolling plan designed for 2 runs/week integrated with 4x/week strength training. After each run, report back with your distance, time, average HR, RPE (1-10), and run/walk ratio used. The plan adjusts based on your performance. Every 4th week is a recovery week. No run is wasted, and no missed run needs to be "made up."

### Why This Split Works: The Interference Effect

Your strength training schedule creates a specific recovery landscape:

```
SAT: Push (upper body)
SUN: Pull (upper body)
MON: Legs + Core  ← heavy lower body
TUE: ZONE 2 RUN   ← 16-20 hrs post-legs = active recovery, easy pace
WED: Upper
THU: Rest
FRI: QUALITY RUN   ← 4 days post-legs = full recovery, high intensity
```

**Tuesday (post-legs):** Running easy 16-20 hours after heavy leg work promotes blood flow to fatigued muscles without adding mechanical stress. Research by Doma et al. (2015) found that low-intensity cardio 12-24 hours after resistance training does not impair strength recovery and may enhance it through increased nutrient delivery. Your legs may feel heavy for the first 8-10 minutes — this is normal and clears as blood flow increases.

**Friday (quality):** By Friday, it has been 4 full days since Monday's leg session. The neuromuscular system is fully recovered — muscle glycogen replenished, no residual fatigue competing for motor unit recruitment. This is the optimal window for high-intensity running. The next leg session is not until Monday, providing 2+ days of recovery.

### The 50/50 Session Split at Low Volume

With only 2 sessions, pure 80/20 polarized training is mathematically impossible. Instead, the plan follows a 50/50 session split (1 easy, 1 hard) which, by training time, approximates 60-65% easy / 35-40% hard. Research from the University of Verona trial confirms that below 4 hours/week training volume, intensity distribution is far less critical than consistency. Your concurrent strength training provides additional training stimulus not captured in the running-only intensity math.

## Phase 1: Return to Running (Weeks 1-10)

**Goal:** Rebuild a running base safely using run/walk intervals. Progress from 2-min run segments to 20-30 minutes of continuous running. All running at Zone 1-2 only. No intensity work.

**Why 10 weeks instead of 8?** With only 2 sessions/week (vs. 3), there are fewer adaptation opportunities per week. The run/walk ratio advances in 0.5-minute increments instead of 1-minute increments to avoid overly aggressive jumps between sessions.

**Progression rule:** Only advance to the next run/walk ratio when the current one feels like 5/10 effort or lower on both sessions that week. If either run feels 7+/10, repeat the current week.

## Plan Adaptation Rules

The plan adjusts based on your reported data:

| Situation | Adjustment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Run/walk session felt 7+/10 effort | Repeat current week's ratio, do not advance |
| Any pain or discomfort during/after run | Drop back one ratio level, reassess |
| Tuesday avg HR > 155 bpm consistently | Reduce pace; may still be fatigued from Monday legs. Consider whether Monday leg session volume needs temporary reduction |
| Tuesday avg HR < 130 bpm | Fitness is ahead of plan, may accelerate progression |
| Friday quality session RPE > 8/10 | Reduce intensity next Friday by one tier (intervals become tempo, tempo becomes easy + strides) |
| Reported effort consistently 4/10 or lower | Ready to advance sooner |
| Missed a run | Never "make it up." With only 2 runs/week, one missed = 50% volume loss. Do NOT combine both sessions into one day. Continue next week. |
| Two consecutive missed weeks | Reset back one week when resuming |
| Illness or pain lasting >3 days | Reset back one full week when resuming |
| Legs excessively heavy on Tuesday for 3+ weeks | Consider reducing Monday Legs + Core volume by ~20% for one mesocycle, or shifting Tuesday run to Wednesday (post-Upper, no leg tax) |
| Friday consistently feels too easy (RPE 3/10) | Ready to increase intensity tier sooner |
| DOMS from Monday prevents Tuesday run | Skip Tuesday, run easy Wed or Thu instead (one-time swap). If chronic, strength programming needs adjustment. |
| HR and RPE diverge by >2 zones | Signals external stress (sleep, dehydration, illness). Default to the MORE conservative reading. |
| Oura readiness below 70 on a running day | Convert to a 30-min brisk walk at Zone 1. Walking still provides aerobic stimulus without mechanical stress. |

## Science References

This plan is informed by the following research:

### Running Frequency and Minimum Effective Dose
1. **Foster et al. (2015)** -- Training intensity distribution matters less at lower weekly volumes (<3.5 hours). At ~1.5-2 hours of weekly running, session consistency is the dominant variable.
2. **University of Verona trial** -- Below 4 hours/week of training, intensity distribution (80/20 vs 50/50) has minimal impact on VO2max improvement.

### Sprint Interval Training at Low Frequency
3. **Gillen et al. (2014)** -- 3x/week SIT (4-6 x 30-sec all-out sprints with 4 min recovery) produced similar VO2max improvements to moderate-intensity continuous training at 5x/week, despite ~5x less total training time.
4. **Gist et al. (2014) meta-analysis** -- SIT protocols showed significant VO2max improvement (mean 7.1% over 4-8 weeks) at frequencies as low as 2-3x/week.
5. **Burgomaster et al. (2008)** -- 6 weeks of SIT produced equivalent improvements in muscle oxidative capacity compared to endurance training, despite 90% less training volume.

### Concurrent Training and the Interference Effect
6. **Hickson (1980)** -- Seminal concurrent training study. Interference is minimized with adequate session separation (>6 hours, ideally 24+).
7. **Wilson et al. (2012) meta-analysis** -- Running produces greater interference than cycling due to eccentric loading. Interference minimized when high-intensity running is not performed within 24 hours of heavy lower-body resistance training.
8. **Doma et al. (2015)** -- Low-intensity running 12-24 hours after resistance training does not impair strength recovery. Supports Tuesday easy running after Monday legs.
9. **Jones et al. (2013)** -- 2 strength sessions + 2 running sessions per week showed no significant interference effect over 12 weeks when separated by 6+ hours.

### Aerobic Base and Zone 2
10. **San Millan & Brooks (2018)** -- Zone 2 training maximizes mitochondrial fat oxidation. 12-16 weeks produces 15-20% mitochondrial volume increase.
11. **Seiler (2010)** -- Polarized training (80% low / 20% high intensity) produces superior endurance adaptations. At low volume, the principle translates to: keep most running easy, make the hard session genuinely hard.

### Running with Concurrent Strength Training Benefits
12. **Beattie et al. (2014)** -- 40 weeks of concurrent strength + endurance training improved running economy by 4.2% vs. endurance-only.
13. **Blagrove et al. (2018) meta-analysis** -- Heavy resistance training improves running economy by 2-8%, primarily through improved neuromuscular function and tendon stiffness.

### Return to Running
14. **Galloway, J.** -- Run-Walk-Run method: 98% completion rate with reduced post-race muscle damage vs. continuous running.
15. **Sports Medicine, 2024 scoping review** -- Graduated walk-run intervals with symptom-guided progression as standard return-to-running protocol after injury.
16. **Karvonen et al., 1957** -- HR reserve method endorsed by ACSM for personalized zone calculation.
